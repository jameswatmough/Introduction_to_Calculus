The trigonometric, or circular, functions find applications anywhere things oscillate. Vibrating strings, water waves, seasonal temperatures or daily variations in light intensity are all examples where periodic functions arise.

2.5.1 Sketching the Basic Trig Functions

Exercise 2.1 \(y = 2 + 3\cos(2x-3)\)

solution

One aid to sketching functions is to evaluate the function at a few points. The trick is picking the best points. Since we know the cosine is zero when its input is a multiple of \(\pi\), we pick values for \(x\) for which \(2x-3\), the input to the cosine, is \(\pi\). That is, solve \(2x-3 = \pi\) for \(x\). We find \(x = \frac{1}{2}(3+\pi)\). Of course this is only one multiple of \(\pi\). We said any multiple, so replace \(\pi\) by \(n\pi\) and let \(n\) be any integer. 2.6 Inverse Trigonometric Functions

The trigonometric functions and their inverses arise in a wide variety of problems involving triangles, lengths of curves and areas of surfaces. Typical problems lead to triangles where the length of one of the sides is variable and one of the sides is fixed.

A variety of notations are used for the inverse trig functions. The inverse of the sine function is denoted by \(\sin^{-1}\) or \(\arcsin\). In computer languages, the short form asin is often used. Similar notations are used for the other standard trig functions.

Example 2.1 Suppose one end of a beam of unit length is lifted a height \(x\) off the ground.

  • Find the horizontal distance between the ends of the beam.
  • Find the angle the beam makes with the ground.

Label the vertical of a right triangle as the dependent variable \(x\) and the hypotenuse with the fixed unit length.

\[x = \sin(y)\] \[\sqrt{1-x^2} = \cos(y)\] \[\tan y = \frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\]

The horizontal distance between the ends of the beam is \(\sqrt{1-x^2}\), based on Pythagorasâ€™ theorem. The angle \(y\) is denoted by the inverse function \(\sin^{-1}(x)\). At this point we do not have an algorithm or rule for computing the inverse sine function, other than by use of a ruler and protractor. We will learn methods for estimating values of this function towards the end of this course.

Example 2.2 Suppose a boat is being pulled towards a dock. Find the horizontal distance between the boat and the dock, the length of the rope played out and the angle the rope makes with the horizontal.

The rope is the hypotenuse of a triangle, and its length is variable. We can assume the height of the rope at the dock is 1 unit.

\[x = \csc(y)\] \[\sqrt{x^2-1} = \cot(y)\]

Example 2.3 A third common scenario involves a balloon rising vertically. Here the base of the triangle is the fixed length and the vertical is variable.

\[x = \tan(y)\] \[\sqrt{1+x^2} = \sec(y)\]

Exercise 2.2 Use the above relations to verify the following strange identities.

\[x = \sin(\cos^{-1}(\sqrt{1-x^2})\] \[x = \cot(\csc^{-1}(\sqrt{x^2-1})\] \[x = \tan(\sec^{-1}(\sqrt{1+x^2})\]
